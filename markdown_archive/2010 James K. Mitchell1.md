# Geotechnical Engineering Considerations for Extraterrestrial Infrastructure Construction\n\n## Authors\nJames K. Mitchell and W. David Carrier III\n\n## Abstract\nSpace exploration is entering a new phase involving manned and unmanned landings on the Moon, Mars, and asteroids. Success requires detailed knowledge of extraterrestrial soils and rocks for foundation design, excavation, underground space utilization, and evaluation of local materials for In-Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU). This paper summarizes current knowledge on compositions, geotechnical properties, and in-situ states of these soils.\n\n## Physical Comparison of Earth, Moon, and Mars (Table 1 Summary)\n- **Gravity**: Moon (1/6 Earth), Mars (1/2.64 Earth).\n- **Surface Temperature extremes**: Moon (-233°C to 123°C), Mars (-89°C to -31°C at Viking 1 site).\n- **Atmosphere**: Moon (0 mb), Mars (4.0 to 8.7 mb).\n- **Soil Composition**: Moon rocks include basaltic volcanic rocks, pristine highlands rocks, and polymict breccias. Martian soil contains silicon, iron, aluminum, and magnesium, with oxidation causing the red color.\n\n## Lunar Geotechnical Properties\n- **Relative Density**: Extremely variable at the surface (0-30%) but increases rapidly to >90% (very dense) below 30 cm.\n- **Shear Strength**: Friction angles are high, ranging from 41-43° at the surface to 52-55° at depths of 30-60 cm.\n- **Compressibility**: Compression index ($C_c$) ranges from 0.3 (loose) to 0.05 (dense).\n- **Stability**: Lunar regolith can support stable vertical excavation walls up to at least 2 m deep.\n\n## Martian Geotechnical Properties\n- **Complexity**: Martian geology is significantly more complex than the Moon's due to past water activity, wind erosion, and tectonic processes.\n- **Soil Nature**: Described by the Pathfinder team as similar to clayey silt with embedded sands, granules, and pebbles.\n- **Bearing Capacity**: While likely site-specific, high foundation loads appear feasible, though 'soft zones' (sinking hazards) have been encountered by rovers.\n\n## In-Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU)\n- **Economic Driver**: Transporting materials from Earth costs ~$100,000 per kg.\n- **Goals**: Short-term goals include oxygen production; long-term goals include metal extraction and the use of lunar/Martian regolith as structural construction materials.
