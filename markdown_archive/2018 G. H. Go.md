# Soil Mechanics in Vacuum Chamber

**Authors:** G. H. Go; J. Lee; H. S. Shin; B. H. Rhu; and H. W. Jin  
**Publication:** Earth and Space 2018, ASCE

## Abstract
The dust vacuum chamber is an essential infrastructure for the verification of space exploration equipment, especially extraterrestrial missions for in situ resource utilization (ISRU). This study presents a new approach in soil mechanics that is particularly applicable to the dust vacuum chamber. One of the main issues in operating the dust vacuum chamber is soil disturbance problems during the evacuation. Thus, the appropriate vacuum rate was suggested to minimize the soil disturbance in the dust vacuum chamber using theoretical and numerical approaches. Firstly, simple analytical solutions were used to provide approximate maximum discharge velocity and evacuation time to finish air flow through a soil specimen at a given vacuum pressure. Then, a two dimensional finite element model was developed which simulates the hydro-mechanical behavior of the soil specimen in a vacuum chamber with respect to different vacuum rates. From the numerical analysis results, optimum guidelines have been proposed to ensure that soil disturbance is minimized.

## Introduction
Space exploration is shifting toward surface missions using rovers and landers. Verification of equipment (e.g., for lunar surface missions) requires performance tests in a Dirty Vacuum Chamber (DVC) that simulates lunar surface conditions. A major problem in DVC operation is soil disturbance during depressurization, which alters the initial condition of the soil bin.

## Poroelasticity Theory
The study applies Biot’s (1941) poroelasticity theory to evaluate hydro-mechanical behavior. The constitutive equation relates stress, strain, and pore air pressure:

$$\sigma = C\epsilon - \alpha_B u_a I$$

Soil disturbance begins when effective stress becomes zero due to seepage stress. This occurs when the seepage force exceeds the soil's dead load. The maximum air velocity limit $q_{max}$ is defined as:

$$q_{max} = \frac{K}{\mu} \cdot \rho_d \cdot g$$

## Finite Element Model
A 2D FE model was set up coupling fluid flow and geomechanics. The soil specimen depth was 0.047m. Initial pressure was set to atmospheric, with depressurization occurring from the top boundary.

## Results and Discussion
As absolute pressure approaches vacuum, air velocity increases rapidly due to exponential pressure gradients, inducing high drag forces. For the given soil properties, $q_{max}$ was calculated as 0.000235 m/s. At a vacuum rate of -0.33 kPa/sec, disturbance occurred 282.5 seconds after start. Reducing the vacuum rate (e.g., to -0.11 kPa/sec) before reaching $q_{max}$ effectively drops the air velocity and maintains soil stability.

## Summary and Conclusion
1. Poroelasticity theory provides insight into hydro-mechanical soil behavior in DVCs.
2. FE models help determine appropriate vacuum rates to prevent soil "boiling" or disturbance.
3. Vacuum rates must be controlled to maintain soil specimen integrity until a maximum allowable pressure (e.g., 2.5 Torr) is reached.
