# Effect of Low-Temperature Fluctuations on Martian Steel Structures: Numerical and Analytical Studies

**Authors:** Yongtao Bai, Jing Li, and Xuhong Zhou  
**Journal:** Journal of Cold Regions Engineering (ASCE)  
**Date:** Published online January 27, 2025  
**DOI:** 10.1061/JCRGEI.CRENG-875

## Abstract
This paper employs numerical simulation methodologies to scrutinize the displacement variations and stress distribution within a full-scale circular arch steel structure subjected to diverse thermal cycles on Mars. The research assesses the influence of severe cryogenic temperature fluctuations (ranging from +20°C to -110°C) on Martian steel infrastructure, providing an empirical foundation for architectural design and material selection for a Martian base.

## Introduction
Mars is a primary candidate for human habitation due to its gravity, atmosphere, and relative proximity. Martian construction materials must resist radiation, temperature fluctuations, and chemical reactions. Steel is highlighted as a viable material due to its low cost, recyclability, and the presence of easily available iron and nickel in a reduced elemental form on the Martian surface, facilitating ISRU-based construction.

## Overview of the Martian Environment
- **Atmosphere:** Tenuous, 1% of Earth's density, resulting in poor heat retention.
- **Diurnal Cycle:** ~24h 39min (one sol).
- **Temperature Fluctuations:** Extreme variances, with simulation parameters set at +20°C maximum and -110°C minimum.
- **Gravity:** 3.73 m/s² (0.38G).

## Structure Form and Design
The study focuses on a single-story habitat module:
- **Geometry:** Semicircular arch (radius 5m, longitudinal span 10m).
- **Material:** Steel (50mm thickness).
- **Interior Volume:** Fulfills NASA's habitat specifications (~93 m² for four astronauts).
- **Shielding:** 3m high regolith layer (simulated as uniform 18.687 kPa external pressure).
- **Internal Pressure:** 101.325 kPa (1 atm).

## Key Findings and Discussion
1. **Structural Integrity:** The 50mm steel structure can withstand 1–10 Martian years of temperature cycles without cracking or structural failure.
2. **Stress Distribution:** Under simple pressure (no thermal cycle), stress is localized on the arch. Under temperature fluctuations, stress shifts and concentrates at the connection between the arch and the floor member.
3. **Displacement:** Thermal expansion and contraction lead to cyclical displacement fluctuations. The maximum displacement of the vault point recorded for 1,374 cycles was ~13.11 mm, compared to 2.70 mm under constant pressure.
4. **Fatigue Damage:** While the structure remains safe, cyclical thermal stress leads to incremental fatigue damage, reducing material stiffness over time.

## Conclusion
The study recommends thickening floor members and optimizing joint designs to mitigate stress concentrations. The findings provide a theoretical reference for future Martian infrastructure design, highlighting the feasibility of steel structures in extreme extraterrestrial environments.
