# A microbial role in the construction of Mono Lake carbonate chimneys?\n\n## Abstract\nLacustrine carbonate chimneys are striking, metre-scale constructions. This study uses correlative microscopy to show that microbes were living around Pleistocene Mono Lake carbonate chimneys during their growth. A plausible interpretation is that benthic cyanobacteria and their associated extracellular organic material (EOM) formed tubular biofilms around rising sublacustrine spring vent waters, binding calcium ions and trapping/binding detrital silicate sediment. Decay of these biofilms would locally have increased calcium and carbonate ion activity, inducing calcite precipitation. These findings suggest that the fabric of these chimneys was microbially mediated, representing a macro-scale target in the exploration for ancient or extraterrestrial life.\n\n## 1. Introduction\nDistinguishing biologically influenced sedimentary rock structures from abiotic ones is a key challenge in astrobiology. Mono Lake, California, is a global site for studying biogeochemical processes creating 'tufa' limestone chimney constructions around sublacustrine vents.\n\n## 2. Methods\n### 2.1 Fieldwork\nFieldwork was conducted in October 2014, targeting Pleistocene chimney structures close to Mono City. Samples were taken from naturally broken pipes.\n### 2.2 Analytical Techniques\n- **XRD Analysis:** Determined bulk mineralogy.\n- **Biomarker Analysis:** Conducted using GC-MS to identify organic signatures.\n- **Microscopy:** Used Focussed Ion Beam (FIB), TEM, SEM-EDS, and NanoSIMS ion mapping to analyze microfossil morphology and chemistry.\n\n## 3. Results\n- **Structure:** Chimneys vary from 3 to 4 m in height and 1.5 to 3 m in width. Internally, they consist of calcitic pipes (30–60 cm high, 3 cm wide) with a central conduit.\n- **Mineralogy:** Bulk rock is ~99% calcite with an average magnesium content of 8.1%. About 1% is quartz and other silicates.\n- **Microfossils:** Ubiquitous filamentous microfossils were found within the calcite growth. Two types were identified: Type 1 (0.8–8 µm diameter, dark sheaths) and Type 2 (10–14.5 µm diameter, segmented trichomes).\n- **Chemistry:** NanoSIMS mapping revealed nitrogen-rich organic material (EOM) associated with the filaments. Silicate nanograins were found trapped within this EOM.\n\n## 4. Discussion\nThe evidence suggests that biofilms provided initial sites for calcite nucleation and encouraged organized crystal growth. The morphology of the pipes is interpreted as resulting from tubular microbial mats ('rolled up' mats) growing around spring vents. This bio-influence controlled the macro-scale fabric, even if subsequent crystal growth was abiotic.\n\n## 5. Conclusions\nThe Mono City chimney pipes are packed with fossil microbial filaments. These results suggest that the 'sticky' EOM produced by cyanobacteria trapped sediment and facilitated mineral precipitation, influencing the chimney fabric at nano- to micro-scales.
