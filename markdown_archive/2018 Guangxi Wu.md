# Fabrication of Flexible Thermoelectric Energy Harvesting System

**Authors:** Guangxi Wu; Ferin Neff; and Xiong (Bill) Yu  
**Publication:** Earth and Space 2018, ASCE  

## ABSTRACT
Thermoelectric energy harvesting is promising to provide sustainable energy source in the extraterrestrial environment. This paper describes procedures incurred in the fabrication of thermoelectric energy harvester on flexible substrates. An electrically parallel structure is proposed, which features higher energy efficiency than the counterpart electrically series structure based on theoretical analyses. As proof of concept, flexible thermoelectric harvesters in electrically parallel and series structures are prepared with thick film printing procedures. The performance of elements and device with different architectures are characterized. Both analytical and experimental comparison imply a promising improvement of energy conversion efficiency by using the electrically parallel structure.

## INTRODUCTION
Thermoelectric modules (TEMs) when used as generators (TEGs) are capable of providing continuous energy for extraterrestrial explorations, where sun light is not always available or intense enough for solar cells to work. For example, the solar brightness on Mars and Jupiter is as weak as 45% and 4%, respectively. Radioisotope thermoelectric generators (RTGs) have received interest due to reliability. However, the naturally existing temperature gradient on spacecraft or planetary surfaces (Mars: 20°C to -73°C; Moon: 123°C to -153°C) has not been effectively leveraged. Covering vehicle or habitat surfaces with flexible TEGs could significantly boost energy provision.

## ELECTRICALLY SERIES VS PARALLEL STRUCTURES
### Electrically Series TEM Efficiency
Conventionally, TEMs are connected in series. The maximum efficiency is defined as:

$$\eta_{max} = \frac{T_H - T_C}{T_H} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{1 + Z\bar{T}_{module}} - 1}{\sqrt{1 + Z\bar{T}_{module}} + \frac{T_C}{T_H}}$$

Where $ZT_{module}$ is the figure-of-merit. Property mismatches between p-type and n-type materials often impair this efficiency.

### Electrically Parallel TEM Efficiency
This paper proposes a structure where legs are connected in parallel both thermally and electrically. In this configuration, the module's figure-of-merit can reach the value of the material with the higher performance, preventing the waste of potential caused by material mismatch.

## FABRICATION PROCESS
**Material Preparation:** $Bi_2Te_3$ (n-type and p-type) was processed into nanocomposites via ball milling to increase $ZT$ by limiting phonon transport through grain boundary scattering.

**Ink Preparation:** Ground TE powder was mixed with an epoxy matrix (EPON Resin 863) at a ratio of 4.5:1.

**Printing:** 
1. Substrate: Flexible Polyimide (Kapton).
2. Electrodes: Commercial gold ink (Ercon E4464) printed using mesh screens.
3. TE Legs: Printed using stencils on an MPM TF-100 Thick Film Printer.
4. Curing: 110°C for 24 hours.

## CONCLUSION
Preliminary results indicate that electrically parallel TEMs are a prospective solution to improve energy conversion efficiency and simplify the fabrication of large-area implementations on flexible substrates for space applications.
