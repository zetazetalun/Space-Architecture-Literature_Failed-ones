# Low-velocity impact response of lunar and Martian regolith simulants: Implications for lunar and Martian surface explorations

**Cite as:** Phys. Fluids 36, 113318 (2024); **doi:** 10.1063/5.0233884
**Submitted:** 18 August 2024; **Accepted:** 3 October 2024; **Published Online:** 5 November 2024

**Authors:** Takuma Ishii, Arata Kioka, Jyh-Jaan Steven Huang, Yoshiki Tabuchi, and Yasuhiro Yamada

## Abstract
The granular mechanics of lunar and Martian regolith remain inadequately understood, impeding progress in successful exploration, landing, drilling, sampling, and construction activities on extraterrestrial surfaces. This study investigates the granular behavior of lunar and Martian regolith under impact conditions using high-fidelity simulants (LHS-1, LMS-1, and MGS-1) and terrestrial silica sand. Findings demonstrate that crater depths of regolith layers for lunar highlands and Martian surfaces are greater than those of lunar mare and terrestrial sand, highlighting significant differences in granular behavior that inform future resource utilization and infrastructure development.

## Results and Discussion
- **Crater Depth Analysis:** Impact crater experiments (112 total) showed that LHS-1 and MGS-1 exhibited nearly identical crater depths, which were significantly deeper than T-8 terrestrial sand. For example, at impact velocities of 40-70 cm/s, LHS-1 crater depths were approximately double those of T-8.
- **Geotechnical Factors:** The resistance to impact is primarily influenced by cohesion, internal friction, and particle shape. Regolith particles have irregular, angular shapes and higher cohesion compared to rounded terrestrial sand grains, leading to more complex granular dynamics.
- **Scaling Laws:** Power-law scaling ($d \propto E_k^\alpha$) showed exponents ranging from 0.417 to 0.672 for various simulants, suggesting higher susceptibility to impact penetration in highland and Martian regolith.

## Conclusion
The study provides critical insights for optimizing landing strategies and foundation designs for lunar and Martian infrastructure. It highlights that the higher porosity and unique angularity of regolith particles result in lower resistance to impact compared to terrestrial materials, necessitating specialized engineering approaches for extraterrestrial surface construction.
