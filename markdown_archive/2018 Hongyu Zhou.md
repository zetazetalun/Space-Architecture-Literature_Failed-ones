# Thermal and Mechanical Properties of Cementitious Composites for Additive Construction of Energy-Saving Habitats

**Authors:** Hongyu Zhou, Adam L. Brooks, Dominic Hanna, and Babak Salarieh
**Affiliation:** Dept. of Civil and Environmental Engineering, Univ. of Alabama in Huntsville, Huntsville, AL 35758, USA
**Published in:** Earth and Space 2018, ASCE

## ABSTRACT
Lightweight cementitious composites (LWCC) are cement based material combined with lightweight fillers. Because of its advantages in reducing both structural weight and thermal conductivity, it has the potential to be used in the construction of terrestrial and extraterrestrial habits. Furthermore, since it has relatively small and spherical shaped aggregates, LWCC is also suitable for extrusion based 3-D printing. In this research, a comprehensive and thorough study was carried out to investigate the effects of different lightweight fillers on both the mechanical and thermal properties of LWCC. Various lightweight fillers including three types of hollow glass microspheres (HGM) and two fly ash cenospheres (FACs) are studied in conjunction with various particle size, shell wall thickness, and volume fractions. Both mechanical and thermophysical properties were tested. The results indicated that the thermal property of LWCC is mostly governed by the volume fraction of LWFs whereas the mechanical properties are heavily affected by the type and property of LWF particles included.

## INTRODUCTION
Provoked by recent advancements in additive manufacturing, 3D printable concrete has been explored as an alternative method to construct concrete structures. 3-D printing of cementitious materials has the potential to address several major challenges: (i) it eliminates formwork, (ii) reduces material waste and human error through automation, and (iii) allows for functionally graded material design.

## RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

### Compressive Strength
The compressive strength of LWCC mortars increases with higher HGM concentrations (up to ~15% volume fraction) due to improved rheology and reduced air content. Beyond 15%, strength decreases as stress cracks propagate through the particle shells. Mortars with smaller HGM particles and thicker shells (e.g., S60) yielded higher mechanical strength. At 28% volume fraction, S60 mortar is 125% stronger than K25 mortar.

### Thermal Properties
Thermal conductivity is primarily dictated by the volume fraction of the lightweight fillers rather than their type. At 7% volume fraction, conductivity reduces by ~30%. As volume fraction approaches 28%, conductivity decreases to 0.65-0.8 W/m·K (compared to 2.1-2.2 W/m·K for the control).

## CONCLUSIONS
1. Thermal properties are primarily dictated by filler volume fraction and are insensitive to the type of filler.
2. Mechanical properties are greatly affected by filler properties (size, shell thickness, crushing strength).
3. Smaller LWF particles are well distributed in the matrix and are suitable for extrusion-based additive construction.
4. Compressive behavior of LWCC is strain-rate sensitive, with dynamic strength being 40-66% higher than quasi-static strength.
